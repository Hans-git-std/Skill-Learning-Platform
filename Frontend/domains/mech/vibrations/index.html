Chapter 1

Fundamentals of Vibrations & SHM

Kinematics of oscillation, periodic motion, phase, and mathematical foundations.

Mechanical vibration is the rigorous study of the oscillatory motions of dynamic systems. A system vibrates when it possesses elasticity (to store potential energy) and mass (to store kinetic energy), and experiences a perturbing force. The continuous interchange of these energy forms yields motion that is periodic or aperiodic in nature.

1. Simple Harmonic Motion (SHM)

The simplest form of periodic motion is Simple Harmonic Motion, characterized by an acceleration that is directly proportional to the displacement and always directed towards the equilibrium position. If we track the projection of a particle moving in a perfect circle at constant angular velocity $\omega$ onto a diameter, its displacement $x(t)$ perfectly describes SHM.

$$ x(t) = A \sin(\omega t + \phi) $$ $$ v(t) = \frac{dx}{dt} = A\omega \cos(\omega t + \phi) $$ $$ a(t) = \frac{dv}{dt} = -A\omega^2 \sin(\omega t + \phi) = -\omega^2 x(t) $$

Here, $A$ represents the absolute amplitude (maximum displacement), $\omega$ is the circular natural frequency in radians per second, and $\phi$ is the phase angle determined by the initial conditions. The period of the oscillation $\tau$ is the time taken to complete one full cycle, while the frequency $f$ in Hertz is the reciprocal of the period.

$$ \tau = \frac{2\pi}{\omega} \quad ; \quad f = \frac{1}{\tau} = \frac{\omega}{2\pi} $$

2. Degrees of Freedom (DOF)

A crucial step in vibration analysis is mathematical modeling, which begins by identifying the number of degrees of freedom. The number of DOFs is defined as the minimum number of independent coordinates required to completely specify the spatial positions of all masses in the system at any given instant in time. A simple pendulum constrained to swing in a single plane has 1 DOF ($\theta$). A rigid car suspended on four independent shock absorbers generally requires 3 DOFs (vertical bounce, pitch, and roll) to describe its rigid-body motion.

Chapter 2

Free Undamped SDOF Systems

Newtonian mechanics, D'Alembert's principle, and Rayleigh's energy methods.

When a system is displaced from its static equilibrium and released, it oscillates purely under the influence of its inherent restoring forces without any continuing external excitation. If we assume energy dissipation (damping) is negligible, the system theoretically oscillates indefinitely. This is known as free undamped vibration.

1. Equation of Motion: Newton's Second Law

Consider a simple spring-mass system. A mass $m$ is attached to a linear spring of stiffness $k$. At static equilibrium, the spring deflection $\delta_{st}$ balances the weight ($mg = k\delta_{st}$). If we displace the mass by a dynamic distance $x$, the restoring force of the spring becomes $k(\delta_{st} + x)$. According to Newton's Second Law ($\sum F = m\ddot{x}$):

$$ mg - k(\delta_{st} + x) = m\ddot{x} \implies -kx = m\ddot{x} $$ $$ m\ddot{x} + kx = 0 $$

This is the classic homogeneous linear second-order ordinary differential equation governing undamped SDOF systems. Dividing by $m$, we isolate the acceleration:

$$ \ddot{x} + \omega_n^2 x = 0 \quad \text{where} \quad \omega_n = \sqrt{\frac{k}{m}} $$

Here, $\omega_n$ is the natural circular frequency. It is a fundamental property of the system entirely independent of the amplitude of vibration. The solution to this differential equation requires two initial conditions: initial displacement $x(0) = x_0$ and initial velocity $\dot{x}(0) = v_0$.

2. Rayleigh's Energy Method

For conservative systems where no energy is lost, the total mechanical energy $E_{total}$ remains perfectly constant. $E_{total} = T + U = \text{Constant}$, where $T$ is the kinetic energy and $U$ is the potential strain energy. Taking the time derivative of the total energy yields the equation of motion:

$$ \frac{d}{dt}(T + U) = 0 $$ $$ \frac{d}{dt}\left(\frac{1}{2}m\dot{x}^2 + \frac{1}{2}kx^2\right) = m\dot{x}\ddot{x} + kx\dot{x} = 0 \implies \dot{x}(m\ddot{x} + kx) = 0 $$

Rayleigh's principle further dictates that the maximum kinetic energy occurs precisely as the system passes through the equilibrium position ($x=0$), and the maximum potential energy occurs at the extreme amplitude positions ($v=0$). By equating $T_{max} = U_{max}$, we can rapidly solve for the natural frequency of highly complex continuous systems by guessing a mode shape.

Chapter 3

Free Damped SDOF Systems

Viscous dashpots, energy dissipation, and the logarithmic decrement.

In reality, energy is always dissipated due to internal friction, fluid drag, or acoustic radiation. We model this dissipation most commonly using linear viscous damping, where the resisting damping force is directly proportional and opposite to the velocity of the mass ($F_c = c\dot{x}$). The constant $c$ is the viscous damping coefficient.

1. The Damped Equation of Motion

Incorporating the damping force into our free-body diagram yields:

$$ m\ddot{x} + c\dot{x} + kx = 0 \implies \ddot{x} + 2\zeta\omega_n\dot{x} + \omega_n^2x = 0 $$

We introduce the dimensionless damping ratio ($\zeta$), defined as the ratio of actual damping $c$ to the critical damping coefficient $c_c = 2m\omega_n = 2\sqrt{km}$. The roots of the characteristic equation are $s_{1,2} = -\zeta\omega_n \pm \omega_n\sqrt{\zeta^2 - 1}$. The value of $\zeta$ completely dictates the dynamic behavior of the system.

2. Damping Regimes

  • Underdamped ($0 \le \zeta < 1$): The system oscillates with decaying amplitude. The roots are complex conjugates. The frequency of oscillation is slightly lower than the natural frequency, defined as the damped natural frequency $\omega_d = \omega_n\sqrt{1-\zeta^2}$.
  • Critically Damped ($\zeta = 1$): The system returns to equilibrium as fast as mathematically possible without overshooting or oscillating. The roots are real and repeated. This is highly desired in artillery recoil mechanisms and high-end automotive suspensions.
  • Overdamped ($\zeta > 1$): The system is heavily restricted (e.g., oscillating in thick molasses). It returns to equilibrium exponentially without oscillating, but takes significantly longer than a critically damped system.

3. Logarithmic Decrement

For an underdamped system, we can experimentally determine the damping ratio by comparing the amplitudes of successive cycles. The natural logarithm of the ratio of any two successive peak amplitudes ($x_1$ and $x_2$) is a constant called the logarithmic decrement ($\delta$).

$$ \delta = \ln\left(\frac{x_1}{x_2}\right) = \frac{2\pi\zeta}{\sqrt{1-\zeta^2}} $$ $$ \text{For small damping } (\zeta < 0.1): \quad \delta \approx 2\pi\zeta $$

Chapter 4

Harmonically Excited SDOF Systems

Forced vibrations, resonance, phase lag, and rotating unbalance.

When a dynamic system is subjected to a continuously fluctuating external force, we enter the realm of forced vibrations. The most common and analytically vital excitation is harmonic force, denoted as $F(t) = F_0 \sin(\omega t)$, where $F_0$ is the force amplitude and $\omega$ is the excitation frequency (which is independent of the system's natural frequency $\omega_n$).

1. Steady-State Response and Resonance

The total response of the system is the superposition of a transient response (which decays to zero due to damping) and a steady-state response, which persists indefinitely at the excitation frequency $\omega$. The equation of motion is:

$$ m\ddot{x} + c\dot{x} + kx = F_0 \sin(\omega t) $$

The steady-state solution takes the form $x_p(t) = X \sin(\omega t - \phi)$. The amplitude $X$ and the phase angle $\phi$ (how far the displacement lags behind the applied force) are highly dependent on the frequency ratio $r = \omega / \omega_n$.

$$ X = \frac{F_0 / k}{\sqrt{(1-r^2)^2 + (2\zeta r)^2}} = \frac{X_0}{\sqrt{(1-r^2)^2 + (2\zeta r)^2}} $$ $$ \phi = \tan^{-1}\left(\frac{2\zeta r}{1-r^2}\right) $$

The term $\frac{X}{X_0}$ is the Magnification Factor (M). As the excitation frequency approaches the natural frequency ($r \to 1$), the denominator shrinks drastically. If damping $\zeta$ is very small, the amplitude $X$ trends towards infinity. This catastrophic amplification of vibration is called Resonance.

2. Rotating Unbalance

A frequent source of harmonic excitation in machinery is rotating unbalance. If an eccentric mass $m_0$ rotates at distance $e$ from the axis with angular velocity $\omega$, it generates a centrifugal force $F_0 = m_0 e \omega^2$. The vertical component of this force is $F(t) = m_0 e \omega^2 \sin(\omega t)$. The resulting amplitude of the main machine mass $M$ becomes frequency-dependent in the numerator as well.

$$ \frac{MX}{m_0 e} = \frac{r^2}{\sqrt{(1-r^2)^2 + (2\zeta r)^2}} $$

Chapter 5

Vibration Isolation & Transmissibility

Force transmission to foundations, base excitation, and mount optimization.

In heavy industrial settings, massive engines generate tremendous vibrational forces. If bolted directly to the floor, these forces transmit unabated into the building structure, causing noise, material fatigue, and damage to sensitive equipment. We introduce resilient mounts (springs and dampers) between the machine and the floor to isolate the vibration.

1. Force Transmissibility

Transmissibility ($T_r$) is the dimensionless ratio of the maximum force transmitted to the foundation ($F_T$) to the amplitude of the disturbing harmonic force ($F_0$). The transmitted force acts through both the spring ($kx$) and the damper ($c\dot{x}$).

$$ T_r = \frac{F_T}{F_0} = \sqrt{\frac{1 + (2\zeta r)^2}{(1-r^2)^2 + (2\zeta r)^2}} $$

Analyzing this equation yields critical design insights. For $T_r$ to be less than 1 (meaning isolation is actually occurring), the frequency ratio $r$ must be strictly greater than $\sqrt{2}$. If $r < \sqrt{2}$, the mounts actually amplify the transmitted force. Furthermore, at very high frequencies ($r \gg \sqrt{2}$), adding damping actually worsens isolation (increases $T_r$), contrary to intuition.

2. Base Excitation (Kinematic Excitation)

Conversely, sometimes the environment vibrates (e.g., a car driving over a corrugated road, or an earthquake), and we must protect the mass (passengers/building). This is base excitation, modeled by a harmonic displacement of the support $y(t) = Y \sin(\omega t)$. The ratio of the mass's absolute displacement amplitude $X$ to the base amplitude $Y$ is mathematically identical to the force transmissibility equation above.

Chapter 6

Two Degree of Freedom (2DOF) Systems

Coupled equations, eigenvalues, mode shapes, and the beat phenomenon.

When a system has two masses capable of independent motion, or a single mass capable of both translation and rotation, it requires two independent coordinates ($x_1, x_2$) to describe its state. This introduces coupling, where the motion of one coordinate directly influences the other.

1. Coupled Equations of Motion

Consider two masses $m_1, m_2$ connected by springs $k_1, k_2, k_3$. Drawing the free-body diagrams yields a system of two coupled differential equations, which are most elegantly written in matrix format using a Mass matrix $[M]$ and a Stiffness matrix $[K]$:

$$ \begin{bmatrix} m_1 & 0 \\ 0 & m_2 \end{bmatrix} \begin{Bmatrix} \ddot{x}_1 \\ \ddot{x}_2 \end{Bmatrix} + \begin{bmatrix} k_1+k_2 & -k_2 \\ -k_2 & k_2+k_3 \end{bmatrix} \begin{Bmatrix} x_1 \\ x_2 \end{Bmatrix} = \begin{Bmatrix} 0 \\ 0 \end{Bmatrix} $$

This is succinctly written as $[M]\{\ddot{x}\} + [K]\{x\} = \{0\}$.

2. Natural Frequencies and Mode Shapes

Assuming harmonic motion $\{x\} = \{X\}\sin(\omega t)$, we substitute into the matrix equation to yield the generalized eigenvalue problem: $([K] - \omega^2[M])\{X\} = \{0\}$. For a non-trivial solution (where amplitudes aren't zero), the determinant of the impedance matrix must be zero:

$$ \det([K] - \omega^2[M]) = 0 $$

Expanding this determinant yields a quadratic equation in $\omega^2$ called the characteristic equation. The two positive roots ($\omega_1, \omega_2$) are the system's two natural frequencies (eigenvalues). Substituting these frequencies back into the matrix yields the amplitude ratios $\{X\}^{(1)}$ and $\{X\}^{(2)}$, known as the Principal Mode Shapes (eigenvectors). In the first mode ($\omega_1$), the masses typically move in-phase. In the second mode ($\omega_2$), they move out-of-phase.

Chapter 7

Dynamic Vibration Absorbers

Tuned Mass Dampers, counter-excitation, and resonance elimination.

When a primary machine (mass $M$) operating at a specific frequency $\omega$ falls into violent resonance due to $F_0 \sin(\omega t)$, altering its heavy mass or stiff foundations might be practically impossible. The brilliant engineering solution is to bolt a much smaller secondary spring-mass system ($m, k$) onto the main machine to act as a Dynamic Vibration Absorber (DVA).

1. Principle of the Undamped Absorber

By adding the absorber, we convert a 1DOF system into a 2DOF system. The amplitude $X_1$ of the primary mass and $X_2$ of the absorber mass are derived from the coupled equations. Remarkably, if we tune the natural frequency of the absorber ($\omega_a = \sqrt{k/m}$) to perfectly match the excitation frequency ($\omega$), the amplitude $X_1$ of the massive primary machine becomes mathematically zero.

$$ X_1 = \frac{(k - m\omega^2)F_0}{(K - M\omega^2)(k - m\omega^2) - k^2} $$

When $k - m\omega^2 = 0 \implies \omega^2 = k/m$, the numerator is zero, thus $X_1 = 0$. The small absorber mass $m$ takes on all the kinetic energy, vibrating vigorously to exert a spring force that exactly cancels out the external excitation force $F_0$.

2. Tuned Mass Dampers (TMD)

While an undamped absorber is perfect for constant-speed machines (like turbines), it introduces two new resonant frequencies. If the machine's speed fluctuates, it might hit these new resonances. By adding a viscous damper to the absorber (creating a TMD), we sacrifice the "perfect zero" amplitude at the tuning frequency to significantly suppress the vibration amplitude across a much wider bandwidth of frequencies. TMDs are famously used in super-tall skyscrapers like Taipei 101 to mitigate wind-induced swaying.

Chapter 8

Multi Degree of Freedom (MDOF) Systems

Matrix formulation, orthogonality of modes, and modal analysis.

Real-world structures, such as multi-story buildings, aircraft wings, and vehicle suspension networks, possess many degrees of freedom. A 3-story building modeled as a shear frame has 3 DOFs. A car has at least 6. Analyzing these requires robust linear algebra and matrix operations.

1. The Eigenvalue Problem in N-Dimensions

The general undamped free vibration equation for an N-DOF system is $[M]\{\ddot{x}\} + [K]\{x\} = \{0\}$. The determinant $\det([K] - \omega^2[M]) = 0$ yields a polynomial of degree N in $\omega^2$. Solving this provides $N$ natural frequencies ($\omega_1 \le \omega_2 \le ... \le \omega_N$). Each frequency corresponds to a unique N-dimensional eigenvector (mode shape) $\{u\}_r$.

2. Orthogonality of Mode Shapes

A profound and immensely useful property of linear systems is that their principal mode shapes are orthogonal (conceptually perpendicular) with respect to both the mass and stiffness matrices. For any two different modes $r$ and $s$ ($r \neq s$):

$$ \{u\}_r^T [M] \{u\}_s = 0 $$ $$ \{u\}_r^T [K] \{u\}_s = 0 $$

This means that energy does not transfer between different modes. They exist entirely independently of one another. We typically normalize these mode shapes so that the modal mass $\{u\}_r^T [M] \{u\}_r = 1$.

3. Modal Superposition Method

Because the mode shapes form an orthogonal basis (like XYZ axes in space), any arbitrary complex deformation of the structure can be expressed as a linear combination of its mode shapes. This allows us to decouple the massive, complex $N \times N$ matrix equation into $N$ separate, easily solvable SDOF equations. We solve them individually, then sum the results back together—a technique vital for software like ANSYS and Abaqus.

Chapter 9

Continuous Systems (Distributed Mass)

The wave equation, infinite DOFs, transverse beams, and nodal points.

Until now, we have assumed lumped parameters (mass concentrated at points, weightless springs). In reality, mass and elasticity are distributed continuously throughout a physical body. A guitar string, a steel beam, or a column of air actually possesses an infinite number of degrees of freedom, and thus, an infinite number of natural frequencies.

1. Transverse Vibrations of a String

Consider a taut string of length $L$, mass per unit length $\rho$, under constant tension $T$. By analyzing a differential element $dx$, Newton's second law yields a partial differential equation known as the 1D Wave Equation:

$$ T \frac{\partial^2 y}{\partial x^2} = \rho \frac{\partial^2 y}{\partial t^2} \implies \frac{\partial^2 y}{\partial x^2} = \frac{1}{c^2}\frac{\partial^2 y}{\partial t^2} $$

Where $c = \sqrt{T/\rho}$ is the velocity of wave propagation. Solving this using separation of variables ($y(x,t) = X(x)T(t)$) subject to boundary conditions (fixed at $x=0$ and $x=L$), yields natural frequencies that are integer multiples of the fundamental frequency: $\omega_n = \frac{n\pi c}{L}$ where $n = 1,2,3...$

2. Transverse Vibrations of a Euler-Bernoulli Beam

A beam resists bending due to its flexural rigidity ($EI$). The partial differential equation is fourth-order in space and second-order in time:

$$ EI \frac{\partial^4 y}{\partial x^4} + \rho A \frac{\partial^2 y}{\partial t^2} = 0 $$

The solution requires four boundary conditions. For a simply supported beam (hinged at both ends), the natural frequencies are proportional to $n^2$, unlike the string. Higher modes show Nodes—specific points along the beam that remain perfectly stationary while the rest of the beam vibrates furiously.

Chapter 10

Whirling of Shafts (Critical Speeds)

Rotor dynamics, gyroscopic effects, and high-speed stability.

In turbomachinery (jet engines, steam turbines, compressors), heavy disks are mounted on high-speed rotating shafts. Due to manufacturing tolerances, the center of mass of the disk is never perfectly aligned with the geometric axis of rotation, creating an initial eccentricity $e$.

1. The Whirling Phenomenon

As the shaft spins at angular velocity $\omega$, centrifugal force bends the shaft. Because the shaft rotates as it bends, the entire bent shaft sweeps out a 3D surface, looking like a skipping rope. This is called whirling. The centrifugal force ($F = M(\delta + e)\omega^2$) is resisted by the shaft's bending stiffness ($kx = M\delta$).

$$ \delta = \frac{e(\omega/\omega_n)^2}{1 - (\omega/\omega_n)^2} = \frac{er^2}{1-r^2} $$

2. Critical Speed

As the rotational speed $\omega$ approaches the lateral natural frequency $\omega_n$ ($r \to 1$), the denominator approaches zero, and the deflection $\delta$ tends toward infinity. This speed is known as the Critical Speed of the shaft. Running a turbine at its critical speed will inevitably shatter the bearings or snap the shaft.

Remarkably, looking at the equation, if the machine can rapidly accelerate past the critical speed into the supercritical region ($r \gg 1$), the denominator becomes negative, and $\delta \approx -e$. The heavy disk essentially inverts and rotates perfectly smoothly around its own center of gravity, with the shaft bent to accommodate it. This is why high-speed jet turbines run incredibly smoothly once they surpass their critical startup speeds.

Chapter 11

Vibration Measurement & Signal Processing

Accelerometers, time-domain, frequency-domain, and Fast Fourier Transform (FFT).

Theoretical analysis must be verified with physical measurement. In modern engineering, we use piezoelectric accelerometers mounted on machinery to capture vibration data. These sensors output a voltage proportional to acceleration in the time domain. However, a raw time-domain signal from a complex machine is often an unreadable, chaotic mess of overlapping waves.

1. The Fourier Transform

The mathematical breakthrough of Jean-Baptiste Joseph Fourier states that any complex periodic waveform can be decomposed into a sum of simple sine and cosine waves of varying frequencies and amplitudes. We use the Discrete Fourier Transform (and its computationally efficient cousin, the FFT) to convert the signal from the Time Domain into the Frequency Domain.

$$ X(f) = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} x(t) e^{-i 2\pi ft} dt $$

2. Spectrum Analysis

When we plot amplitude against frequency (a spectrum), the chaotic noise separates into distinct sharp peaks. Each peak represents a specific component vibrating at that frequency. For example, in a gearbox, a peak exactly at the gear mesh frequency indicates healthy operation, but sidebands around that peak strongly indicate a cracked gear tooth. This makes FFT the most powerful tool in predictive maintenance and condition monitoring.
